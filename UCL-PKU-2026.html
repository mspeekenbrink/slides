Hidden Markov models

An extension of mixture models, combining unsupervised clustering with transitions between clusters (states) over time (Visser & Speekenbrink, 2022)

HMMs assume time series of observed variables \(Y_{1:T} = (Y_1, \ldots, Y_T)\) reflect an underlying sequence of latent states \(S_{1:T} = (S_1, \ldots, S_T)\). They are defined by

  • Initial state distribution \(p(S_1 = s)\)
  • State-conditional response distributions \(p(Y_t|S_t = s)\)
  • State transition distributions \(p(S_{t+1}|S_{t})\)

The state transition distribution assumes that the next state depends solely on the immediately preceding state. This is the (first-order) Markov assumption

HMMs are used in a wide variety of fields (e.g., speech recognition, computational biology, economics).

Steps in hidden Markov modelling

Flexible hidden Markov modelling is straightforward with the R package depmixS4. You can define flexible models for the initial states (state at \(t=1\)), state-transition models, and state-conditional response models.

  • Choose the number of latent states (or try models with different numbers of states)
  • Define submodels for the initial states, state transitions, and state-conditional responses
  • Estimate the model parameters (here usually maximum likelihood by EM algorithm)
  • Determine latent state for each time point by global or local decoding

Symptom networks

Repeated games

  • Repeated games offer a controlled “micro-cosmos” to study the dynamics of human interaction. In contrast to single-shot games, repeated games allow for reputation formation.
  • Traditionally, game theory has focused on deriving optimal policies:
    • Subgame perfect equilibrium (SPE) extends Nash equilibrium to repeated and stochastic games.
    • Folk Theorems: Anything goes?
  • Psychology and behavioural economics focus on how people actually play repeated games. Observed behaviour deviates from predictions for self-interested rational agents
    • Social utility function (Fehr & Schmidt, 1999); psychological game theory (Battigalli & Dufwenberg, 2022)?

The Repeated Trust Game

King-Casas et al. (2008) let \(n=186\) play the repeated Trust Game for \(T=10\) rounds. Participants in the trustee role included \(n=55\) people with Borderline Personality Disorder.

RTG

  • The investor gets an endowment of 20 and decides how much to invest in the trustee.
  • The investment gets tripled, and the trustee decides how much to send back to the investor, keeping the remainder for themselves.
  • The investor gains \(R^{(I)} = 20 - A^{(I)} + A^{(T)}\) and the trustee gains \(R^{(T)} = 3 \times A^{(I)} - A^{(T)}\), with \(A^{(I)} \in (0, \ldots, 20)\) and \(A^{(T)} \in (0, \ldots, 3 \times A^{(I)})\).

Conclusion

::::

  • Hidden Markov models offer a flexible framework to analyse sequences of behaviour
    • State-conditional response distributions \(p(Y_t|S_t)\) can take any form, including multivariate Normal distributions, categorical distributions, generalized linear models, etc.
    • Software such as depmixS4 provides access to a full range of HMMs
  • First-order Markov assumption may seem restrictive, but
    • State transitions can depend on covariates (including previous behaviours)
    • In principle, it is possible to rewrite an \(n\)th-order Markov into a first-order Markov model
  • Identifying the optimal/true number of latent states is not necessarily easy. Ideally, states have a clear psychological meaning.
